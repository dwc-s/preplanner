# Fire Service Pre-Incident Planning Software

## Market Survey: Pricing, Features, and Awarded Contract Values

*Compiled July 2026*

---

## How to Read This Report

Vendors are sorted into three tiers by **evidence quality**, not by product quality:

- **Tier 1** — Pricing published by the vendor or documented in an executed public contract.
- **Tier 2** — Real, verifiable vendor. Pricing model documented, but no dollar figures available publicly.
- **Tier 3** — Named by software-listing aggregators, but could not be independently verified. **Treat all Tier 3 pricing as unreliable.**

Every source is linked in the References section. Where a figure came from a municipal agenda packet or vendor quote, the source document is cited directly.

## Tier 3 — Named by Aggregators, Unverified

The following appear across AI-generated software listicles (gitnux, wifitalents, zipdo) with confident pricing attached. **None could be independently confirmed as real, currently-operating products.**

> Cadasio · FireFacilities PreFirePro · Response411 · Ignite Preplan (Vision Pioneer) · PrePlanIT · Vector One · Reach PrePlan · Response Plan Center · OptiMapper · FirePrePlans · SiteMapper · 360 Precision · FireView360 · DeskCATS · FirePrograms Spectra

### Why These Are Excluded

The listicles contradict each other on basic facts. Response411 is variously described as:

- Free for first responders, facilities paying $29–99/month per site [28]
- Starting at ~$500/year, scaling with users [29]
- Annual subscriptions starting at $495 [30]

Three different pricing models for the same product across three sites — each labeled "Official docs verified." The same pages also list **AutoCAD** and **Esri** as "fire preplan software," which indicates the depth of curation involved. All carry affiliate-commission disclosures.

**Do not cite Tier 3 pricing in a procurement document.** If one of these products is genuinely of interest, confirm it exists on the vendor's own domain first.

## Analysis

### Four Pricing Models — Not Interchangeable

The structural model matters more than the headline number:

| Model | Vendors | Favors |
|---|---|---|
| Per-department, size-scaled | First Due, FlowMSP | Middle of the range; most common |
| Per-call-volume, unlimited seats | OnScene Xplorer | Low-volume rural departments with many users |
| Per-station | FireHouse Manager | Departments with many users per station |
| Per-preplan | Blazemark | Departments with few target hazards |

A small department with a large commercial building inventory gets the worst outcome from per-preplan pricing and the best from per-call-volume pricing. **This structural fit is a bigger cost driver than any discount negotiable with a single vendor.**

### Escalation Clauses Outweigh Sticker Price

- **First Due:** contract permits renewal increases of **up to 5% per year**, with automatic 12-month renewals. [4]
- **ESO:** invoices may carry annual uplift *plus* usage-overage increases, and **paying the invoice constitutes acceptance**. [17]

Over a five-year term, that gap can exceed the difference between initial quotes. Belmont FD's own analysis noted **historical 3–5% annual increases across all their software vendors**. [1] Assume escalation will happen; get it capped in writing.

### The Consolidation Argument Drives Most Procurements

Belmont FD replaced four separate products with one contract at essentially flat cost: [1]

| Prior Software | 2024 Cost | Renewal Cost |
|---|---|---|
| Emergency Reporting | $3,713.60 | $5,500.00 |
| Aladtec (scheduling) | $2,911.40 | $2,911.40 |
| Lexipol (training) | $3,125.00 | $3,125.00 |
| Active911 (call alerting) | $750.00 | $750.00 |
| **Total** | **$12,286.40** | — |
| **First Due all-inclusive** | — | **$12,050.00** |

One vendor, one invoice, no net cost increase. This — not pre-planning features specifically — is the pattern behind most of these procurements. New Berlin, WI made the same move for the same stated reason. [4]

### The NERIS Deadline Has Passed

NFIRS was replaced by NERIS, and reporting to the legacy system ended **January 1, 2026**. [31] The buyer's-market window — when vendors competed aggressively for migration business — is largely closed. Departments still running non-compliant systems now negotiate from a weak position.

### The Esri Option Deserves More Weight Than $0 Suggests

If the municipality already holds an ArcGIS license (many do through countywide agreements), the Pre-Incident Planning solution deploys into the existing organization at no additional license cost. [8][9]

Brevard County Fire Rescue replaced an Excel-and-email workflow with it and **cut process time to roughly a third**, using an ArcGIS Experience Builder app for district chiefs to organize plans and manage site visits. [32]

Caveats are real: the solution **requires specific technical experience and software**, and at least one deployment version has moved to **mature support**. [9] Confirm whether the municipal GIS department already holds the license before pricing commercial alternatives against it.

## Recommended Next Steps

1. **Check existing ArcGIS licensing** with the municipal/county GIS department before anything else. It may reframe the entire budget question.

2. **File public records requests** with two or three comparable-size departments for their current fire RMS contract *and* their most recent invoice. Executed contracts are public records; in Massachusetts, under MGL c.66. The invoice matters as much as the contract — it shows what escalation clauses have actually done to the price over time, which the initial award never reveals.

3. **Request the Harris County ESD No. 7 award record.** They ran a formal RFP for Pre-Incident Planning Services with proposals due February 10, 2025, evaluated by a committee including the Deputy Chief of Operations, with award recommended to the Board of Commissioners. [33] That award is public and is one of the few competitive pre-planning-specific procurements on record.

4. **Pull the Nevada, MO ESO quote** manually — it is the most granular ESO line-item breakdown located, but blocks automated access. [17]

5. **Match the pricing model to the department profile** before comparing quotes. Ask each vendor to quote under their standard model *and* state the escalation cap in writing.

## Methodology Note

Vendor-published pricing and executed municipal contracts were prioritized over software-listing aggregators. Where an aggregator was the only source for a product's existence, that product was placed in Tier 3 and its pricing excluded from analysis. Two source documents ([2] and [17]) block automated retrieval and were cited from search-result extracts; both are worth retrieving manually, as each contains a full itemized vendor quote.

Contract values reflect the terms at time of award and do not account for subsequent escalation.
